# North American Wood Fence Design and Lumber Catalog

**Research specification for Fence Planner**  
**Coverage:** common residential, acreage, garden, and light agricultural wood-fence systems in the United States and Canada  
**Prepared:** July 16, 2026

## 1. Scope and naming rule

This catalog covers fences whose visible structure is primarily wood. It also includes wood-framed welded-wire fencing because it is commonly bought and built with dimensional lumber. Vinyl, composite, chain-link, ornamental metal, and masonry fences need separate component catalogs.

The word **style** is used loosely in retail catalogs. A precise fence description has four independent layers:

1. **Construction pattern:** solid board, board-on-board, shadowbox, spaced picket, horizontal slat, tongue-and-groove, basketweave, louver, rail, or wire infill.
2. **Board or picket profile:** flat, dog-ear, pointed, Gothic, or French Gothic.
3. **Panel top line:** straight, convex/arched, concave/scalloped, or stepped.
4. **Finish package:** exposed rails, cap, cap-and-trim, picture frame, lattice topper, kickboard, and post caps.

These layers should not be stored as mutually exclusive fence types. For example, a technically complete description could be:

> 6-foot vertical board-on-board Western red cedar privacy fence, flat top, cap-and-trim finish, 1x6 fence boards, three 2x4 backer rails, and 4x4 ground-contact posts at no more than 8 feet on center.

Master Halco's North American catalog uses the broad families privacy, semi-privacy, lattice-top, picket, ranch rail, and post-and-rail; Home Depot separately filters panel design, panel-top shape, and picket-top style. That separation is the basis of this taxonomy.

## 2. Technical vocabulary

| Technical term | What it means | Typical store description |
|---|---|---|
| **Fence run** | One continuous straight length of fence. | Not a purchased part. |
| **Bay / section** | The fence between two adjacent posts. | Often approximately 6 or 8 feet wide. |
| **Panel** | A factory- or shop-assembled unit containing its own rails and infill. | `6 ft. H x 8 ft. W wood fence panel` |
| **Line post** | Intermediate post in a straight run. | `4x4 pressure-treated ground-contact fence post` |
| **End/terminal post** | Post at the end of a run. | Same lumber as a line post unless using a routed rail system. |
| **Corner post** | Post joining two runs. Routed systems require corner-specific mortises. | `corner post`, or ordinary 4x4/6x6 for face-fastened rails |
| **Gate post** | Post carrying a gate hinge or latch load. | Commonly 6x6 or an engineered steel gate post; size must match the gate design. |
| **Rail / backer rail / stringer** | Horizontal structural member connecting posts and receiving vertical pickets. **Rail** is the preferred generic term; `stringer` is common trade language. | `2x4x8 pressure-treated fence rail` or `cedar backer rail` |
| **Fence board / picket** | Vertical infill board fastened to rails. `Picket` can mean either an open-fence picket or a privacy board, depending on the supplier. | `5/8 in. x 5-1/2 in. x 6 ft. fence picket` or `1x6x6 fence board` |
| **Slat** | Narrow infill member, especially when horizontal or louvered. | `1x4 cedar board`, `1x6 cedar board`, or `5/4x6 deck board` |
| **Base board** | First layer of boards in board-on-board or board-and-batten construction. | Usually the same 1x6 fence board as the cover layer. |
| **Cover board / overlap board** | Second full-width board covering a gap between base boards in board-on-board construction. | `1x6 fence board` |
| **Batten** | Narrow strip covering a joint or stiffening horizontal boards; not a full-width overlap board. | `1x2`, `1x3`, or ripped cedar strip |
| **Nailer / cleat** | Member that gives board ends or edges a fastening surface. | `2x2`, `2x4`, or proprietary post channel |
| **Stile** | Vertical member in a framed panel or gate. | Commonly 1x4, 2x4, or a proprietary milled member. |
| **Cap rail / top cap** | Board laid flat over the top edge of the fence. It sheds water and finishes the top. | `2x6x8` or `2x8x8 cedar cap board` |
| **Fascia / trim board** | Face-mounted finish board below a cap or around a panel perimeter. | `1x4x8 cedar trim board` |
| **Kickboard / rot board / gravel board** | Replaceable horizontal board at the bottom, used to protect infill from soil, trimmers, and impact. It is not the same as a structural rail. | `2x6` or `2x8 ground-contact pressure-treated board` |
| **Lattice** | Intersecting thin wood strips in square or diagonal openings. | `4x8 pressure-treated or cedar wood lattice panel` |
| **Post cap** | Separate cap covering the end grain at the top of a post. | Flat, pyramid, ball, or solar post cap sized to the **actual** post. |
| **On center (O.C.)** | Distance from the center of one post to the center of the next. | Layout dimension, not clear opening. |

## 3. Lumber sizes: what to say at a home center

Dimensional lumber is normally called by its **nominal** size even though its surfaced size is smaller. Fence pickets are frequently sold by actual thickness and width instead. Always read the product tag and measure a sample before calculating coverage.

| Ask for | Common surfaced actual size | Typical fence use |
|---|---:|---|
| 1x2 | 3/4 x 1-1/2 in. | Batten, lattice retainer, light trim |
| 1x3 | 3/4 x 2-1/2 in. | Batten, trim |
| 1x4 | 3/4 x 3-1/2 in. | Picket, fascia, stile, seam cover |
| 1x6 | 3/4 x 5-1/2 in. | Fence board, slat, cover board |
| 1x8 | 3/4 x 7-1/4 in. | Wide fascia or trim |
| 5/4x6 | commonly about 1 x 5-1/2 in. | Stiffer horizontal slat or deck board |
| 2x2 | 1-1/2 x 1-1/2 in. | Nailer, lattice frame, channel, weaver |
| 2x4 | 1-1/2 x 3-1/2 in. | Privacy/picket rail, frame, horizontal stiffener |
| 2x6 | 1-1/2 x 5-1/2 in. | Ranch rail, cap, heavy frame, kickboard |
| 2x8 | 1-1/2 x 7-1/4 in. | Ranch rail, cap, kickboard |
| 4x4 | 3-1/2 x 3-1/2 in. | Typical line/end post |
| 4x6 | 3-1/2 x 5-1/2 in. | Ranch or heavier post |
| 6x6 | 5-1/2 x 5-1/2 in. | Gate, corner, tall/heavy, or premium post |
| Retail fence picket | commonly 5/8 x 5-1/2 in.; 11/16- and 3/4-in. products also exist | Economy privacy or spaced-picket infill |

Two important exceptions:

- Rough-sawn and fence-grade cedar may be closer to full nominal size or follow special dressed dimensions. The Western Red Cedar Lumber Association lists common cedar posts as 4x4 and 6x6 and common rails as 2x4 and 2x6, but its dressed cedar dimensions can differ from ordinary framing lumber.
- Tongue-and-groove boards must be purchased by **face coverage**, not nominal width. A nominal 1x6 profile may cover materially less than 5-1/2 inches after the tongue is engaged.

## 4. Shared construction baseline

These are estimating defaults, not a universal structural code:

- A conventional 6-foot vertical privacy bay is commonly estimated at **8 feet O.C. maximum**, with **three 2x4 rails** and **1x6x6 fence boards**.
- A fence up to 4 feet high commonly uses two rails. Home Depot says to add a middle rail above 4 feet; Lowe's describes the rule of thumb as one stringer for each 2 feet of post height.
- Horizontal-board fences commonly use **6-foot post centers**, especially with thin 1x boards. A vertical center stiffener/batten is prudent and one manufacturer recommends it when a span exceeds 6 feet.
- Post length cannot be selected from fence height alone. Embedment must satisfy local code, frost depth, soil, wind exposure, and the footing system. Retail guidance commonly uses about one-third of the post below grade and requires holes below the local frost line where applicable.
- Keep untreated or above-ground-rated infill clear of soil. Retail instructions commonly show at least 2 inches under pickets; the lower structural rail is commonly kept about 6 inches above grade unless a ground-contact kickboard is part of the design.
- In the United States, specify **AWPA UC4A or higher** for wood in ordinary ground contact and **UC3B** for uncoated above-ground exterior members. In Canada, the corresponding common calls are **CSA O80 UC4.1** for ordinary ground-contact posts and **UC3.2** for uncoated above-ground exposure. Buy by the treatment end tag, not by color.
- Field-treat cuts and holes in preservative-treated wood with the treatment manufacturer's approved end-cut preservative.
- Use stainless-steel or hot-dip-galvanized fasteners/connectors approved for the preservative. Do not treat bright/electroplated fasteners as equivalent to hot-dip galvanized hardware.

### Standard estimating modules used below

- **V8:** 6 ft high x 8 ft O.C. vertical privacy bay; three 2x4 rails.
- **P8:** 4 ft high x 8 ft O.C. spaced-picket bay; two 2x4 rails.
- **H6:** 6 ft high x 6 ft O.C. horizontal-board bay; board ends supported at both posts plus a vertical center stiffener when required.
- **R8:** 8 ft O.C. post-and-board/ranch bay.
- **SR:** one proprietary split-rail bay using the selected manufacturer's 8- or 11-ft rail system.

For a straight run, the first bay needs two posts and each added bay needs one additional post. A run of `N` bays therefore normally needs `N + 1` posts before gates, house connections, or shared corners are resolved.

## 5. Common construction systems

### 5.1 Side-by-side solid-board vertical privacy

**Common names:** solid board, vertical privacy, side-by-side privacy, closeboard in some regions. `Board-to-board` is used informally but is not the clearest purchasing term.

**Exact construction:** Vertical fence boards are fastened on one face of two or three horizontal rails. Each board edge meets the next board edge; there is no deliberate design gap and no second cover layer. Green or wet treated boards are often installed tight because they shrink. Dry boards may require a small movement allowance appropriate to the product.

**V8 wood schedule:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 post per added bay, plus the starting post;
- 3 pieces 2x4x8 rail/backer;
- 18 pieces 1x6x6 fence board when the actual face is about 5-1/2 inches;
- optional 1 piece 2x6x8 ground-contact kickboard, which reduces the required picket length or changes rail placement.

Home Depot's component guide uses 18 1x6x6 pickets per 8-foot stockade/solid-board section. For a between-post panel, calculate from the clear opening rather than blindly using 18.

**Store request:** `4x4 ground-contact posts, 2x4x8 pressure-treated or cedar fence rails, and 5/8 x 5-1/2 x 6-foot or 1x6x6 fence boards.`

**Privacy:** high initially, but narrow gaps can develop from drying and seasonal movement.

### 5.2 Stockade privacy

**Common names:** stockade, pointed stockade, dog-ear stockade.

**Exact construction:** Structurally this is usually the side-by-side solid-board system. The distinguishing feature is a row of closely set vertical pickets with pointed, dog-ear, or occasionally Gothic tops. In retail use, `stockade panel` can also simply mean a preassembled solid privacy panel.

**V8 wood schedule:** Same as side-by-side solid board: one post allocation, three 2x4 rails, and approximately 18 1x6x6 shaped pickets per bay.

**Store request:** Add the picket profile: `1x6x6 pointed stockade pickets` or `5/8 x 5-1/2 x 6-foot dog-ear fence pickets.`

**Modeling rule:** Treat stockade as `solid vertical construction + picket-top profile`, not as a fundamentally different framing system.

### 5.3 Vertical board-on-board privacy

**Common names:** board-on-board, overlapping board, double-layer privacy. Regional supplier terminology is inconsistent and some catalogs use `shadowbox` for an overlapping pattern, so describe the layers when ordering.

**Exact construction:** A first course of vertical **base boards** is fastened to one face of the rails with deliberate gaps. A second course of full-width **cover boards** is fastened on the **same face**, centered over those gaps. Each cover board overlaps both neighboring base boards. This is a two-layer fence on one side of the rail framework; it is not the alternating-face shadowbox pattern.

**V8 wood schedule:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 or 6x6 post per added bay;
- 3 pieces 2x4x8 rail/backer;
- 19 pieces 1x6x6 is a common retail estimating baseline;
- use about 21-23 pieces when the specification requires roughly 1 inch of overlap on both edges after drying;
- optional cap-and-trim package.

There is no single correct board count without actual board width and required overlap. Use the formula in Section 8. The Home Depot component guide lists 19 1x6x6 boards per 8-foot section, while a robust 1-inch-per-edge overlap specification normally consumes more.

**Store request:** `1x6x6 fence boards for both the base-board and cover-board courses; three 2x4x8 backer rails; 4x4 or 6x6 ground-contact posts.`

**Privacy:** very high and more tolerant of shrinkage than side-by-side boards.

### 5.4 Shadowbox / good-neighbor / alternating-board fence

**Common names:** shadowbox, alternating board, alternating picket, good-neighbor. `Good-neighbor` can also describe any fence with equivalent finished faces, so confirm the alternating pattern.

**Exact construction:** Vertical boards alternate between opposite faces of the same rails. A board on one face is centered opposite the gap between two boards on the other face. The offset blocks a straight perpendicular sightline while allowing air through the staggered openings. At an oblique angle, the fence is not fully private.

**V8 wood schedule:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 or 6x6 post per added bay;
- 3 pieces 2x4x8 rail/backer;
- about 19 pieces 1x6x6 fence board as a common retail baseline;
- optional cap or cap-and-trim package.

**Store request:** `1x6x6 fence boards for an alternating-face shadowbox fence, three 2x4x8 rails, and ground-contact posts.`

**Privacy:** medium-high straight on; lower from an angle. Both sides show boards rather than one side showing only rails.

### 5.5 Spaced vertical picket fence

**Common names:** picket, open picket, spaced picket, decorative picket.

**Exact construction:** 1x4 or 1x6 vertical pickets are fastened to two horizontal rails with uniform open gaps. Typical residential height is 3-4 feet, though taller semi-privacy versions exist. The top profile and overall top line are independent choices.

**P8 example with 1x4 pickets and 2-1/2-inch gaps:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 post per added bay;
- 2 pieces 2x4x8 rail;
- approximately 16 pieces 1x4x4 picket;
- optional cap rail only if the design is framed for it.

**P8 example with 1x6 pickets and 2-1/2-inch gaps:** approximately 12 pieces 1x6x4 picket.

Use the spaced-board formula in Section 8 because small changes in gap and clear bay width change the count.

**Store request:** `1x4x4 flat-top/French-Gothic/pointed cedar pickets` or the selected 1x6 profile, plus `2x4x8 fence rails` and `4x4 ground-contact posts.`

**Visibility:** open. This is a boundary/decorative fence, not a privacy fence.

### 5.6 Board-and-batten privacy

**Common names:** board-and-batten, batten-board, vertical batten privacy.

**Exact construction:** Wide vertical base boards are installed edge-to-edge or with narrow gaps. Narrow vertical **battens** cover the joints or gaps. This differs from board-on-board because the cover pieces are narrow 1x2 or 1x3 strips rather than another full-width 1x6 fence board.

**V8 example, edge-to-edge base course:**

- 1 allocated post;
- 3 pieces 2x4x8 rail;
- approximately 18 pieces 1x6x6 base board;
- approximately 17 pieces 1x2x6 or 1x3x6 batten, plus end treatment selected by the design;
- optional cap-and-trim package.

If the base boards are deliberately spaced, recalculate both board pitch and batten overlap. A batten must remain wide enough to cover the dry-season gap and still overlap both base-board edges.

**Store request:** `1x6 vertical fence boards with 1x2 or 1x3 cedar battens, three 2x4 backer rails, and ground-contact posts.`

**Privacy:** high when the battens maintain joint coverage.

### 5.7 Framed tongue-and-groove privacy

**Common names:** tongue-and-groove, T&G privacy, V-match/V-joint cedar, framed privacy panel.

**Exact construction:** Milled boards interlock edge-to-edge by a tongue entering the adjacent groove. The field of boards is retained in a perimeter frame or fastened to backers, commonly with 1x2/1x4 side trim and heavier top/bottom rails. Boards may run vertically or horizontally. This is a precision-milled system, not a substitute name for ordinary butted fence pickets.

**Typical V8 wood schedule:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 or 6x6 post;
- 2 or 3 structural 2x4/2x6 rails according to the panel design;
- enough 1x6 T&G boards to cover the clear opening, calculated using the manufacturer's published face coverage;
- 1x2 or 1x4 perimeter retainers/trim, or proprietary grooved rails;
- optional cap, lattice topper, or picture frame.

New England Cedar Fence specifies 1x6 T&G V-match boards in both vertical and horizontal privacy styles, demonstrating why the exact profile must be named when ordering.

**Store request:** `exterior cedar 1x6 tongue-and-groove V-match fence boards, with the actual installed face coverage stated`, plus the matching frame/retainer stock.

**Privacy:** high, with better joint coverage than plain side-by-side boards when the profile is installed for wood movement.

### 5.8 Cap, cap-and-trim, and picture-frame privacy

**Common names:** capped privacy, cap-and-trim, picture frame, framed board fence.

**Exact construction:** These are finish packages applied to solid board, board-on-board, shadowbox, or T&G infill.

- **Cap only:** one 2x6 or 2x8 board lies flat along the top.
- **Cap-and-trim:** the cap is paired with a face-mounted 1x4 or 1x6 fascia directly below it on one or both faces.
- **Picture frame:** top, bottom, and vertical side trim/stiles form a visible rectangle around each infill field.

**Typical add-on per 8-foot bay:**

- cap only: 1 piece 2x6x8 or 2x8x8;
- cap-and-trim: cap plus 1 piece 1x4x8 fascia per finished face;
- full picture frame: add top and bottom 1x4/1x6 trim and vertical 1x4/1x6 stiles at the bay edges on each displayed face.

**Store request:** Name the base fence first, then say `2x6 cedar top cap with 1x4 cedar fascia/trim` or `full 1x4 picture-frame trim.`

**Modeling rule:** Cap-and-trim is not an infill pattern. It must be combinable with several base construction patterns.

### 5.9 Lattice-top privacy

**Common names:** lattice-top, privacy with lattice topper, square-lattice top, diagonal-lattice top.

**Exact construction:** A solid lower fence, commonly 4-1/2 to 5 feet high, is capped by a framed 12-24-inch lattice field. The lattice is captured in wood channels/retainers or sandwiched between trim so its thin strips do not act as the structural frame. Square and diagonal lattice are pattern choices; `privacy lattice` means smaller openings.

**Typical 6-foot V8 schedule:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 or 6x6 post tall enough for the full fence and embedment;
- 3 structural rails or a designed lower-panel frame;
- approximately 18 shorter 1x6 vertical boards for the solid field;
- one 8-foot-wide strip cut from a 4x8 exterior wood lattice sheet at the selected topper height;
- 2x2 or 1x2 top/bottom lattice retainers and vertical end retainers;
- 1 piece 2x4x8 or 2x6x8 top framing/cap member as required by the detail.

Do not count a 4x8 sheet as one topper unless waste is modeled. A 12-inch topper can theoretically yield four 12x96-inch strips from a 4x8 sheet before kerf and damage; a 24-inch topper can yield two.

**Store request:** `exterior cedar or pressure-treated wood lattice sheet, square/diagonal and standard/privacy opening, plus 1x2 or 2x2 lattice cap/retainer moulding.`

**Privacy:** high in the lower field; partial at the topper.

### 5.10 Solid horizontal-board privacy

**Common names:** horizontal privacy, modern horizontal, horizontal slat, contemporary horizontal.

**Exact construction:** Boards run horizontally between or across vertical posts. In a between-post installation, 2x2 nailers/channels on the posts support board ends. In a face-mounted installation, boards fasten across post faces and a vertical 1x4 cover board can conceal butt joints. A center vertical batten/nailer limits bowing. Thin boards require closer support than vertical pickets.

**H6 example using 1x6 boards:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 or 6x6 post per added bay;
- 13-14 pieces 1x6x6 horizontal board for a nominal 72-inch field, depending on exact top/bottom clearances and actual width;
- 2x2 or 2x4 vertical end nailers/channels;
- 1 piece 1x4x6, 2x2x6, or 2x4x6 center stiffener/batten;
- optional 1x4x6 vertical seam cover at each visible post and a 2x6 top cap.

For greater stiffness, specify 5/4x6 boards rather than thin 5/8-inch pickets. Everlasting Fence documents 1x2, 1x4, and 1x6 Western red cedar horizontal boards and describes 6-foot post centers as standard. It also documents face-mounted boards with a vertical seam cover and between-post boards fastened to 2x2s.

**Store request:** `1x6 cedar boards or 5/4x6 cedar/treated deck boards for horizontal fence slats, 4x4 or 6x6 ground-contact posts at 6 feet O.C., and vertical 2x2/2x4 nailers.`

**Privacy:** a plain butted version can open gaps as boards dry. Use T&G, shiplap, or horizontal board-on-board when durable zero-sightline privacy is required.

### 5.11 Spaced horizontal slat fence

**Common names:** horizontal spaced, horizontal slat, modern semi-privacy, open horizontal.

**Exact construction:** Same support system as solid horizontal, but each course has a deliberate, consistent vertical gap. Common documented gaps are 1/4, 1/2, 3/4, and 1 inch.

**H6 examples with 1x6 boards:**

- at a 1/4-inch gap: about 13 rows over a 72-inch field;
- at a 1/2-inch gap: about 13 rows if the courses must cover the full field, or 12 with intentional top/bottom margins;
- at a 1-inch gap: about 12 rows if the courses must cover the full field, or 11 with intentional top/bottom margins;
- plus end nailers/channels and a center stiffener as described above.

Final counts depend on whether the design centers a partial reveal at the top and bottom. Use the horizontal-course formula in Section 8.

**Store request:** Same as solid horizontal, with the exact spacer dimension added: `1x6 horizontal cedar slats with 1/2-inch open gaps.`

**Privacy:** semi-private; airflow and visibility increase with gap size.

### 5.12 Horizontal board-on-board or horizontal shadowbox

**Common names:** horizontal board-on-board, horizontal overlap, horizontal shadowbox.

Two different patterns are sold under these names, so the layer relationship must be stated:

- **Horizontal board-on-board:** spaced horizontal base courses on one plane, with second-plane cover courses centered over the gaps on the same visible side. This is the horizontal equivalent of vertical board-on-board and gives full sightline coverage.
- **Horizontal shadowbox:** courses alternate between opposite sides of vertical supports, producing airflow and an alternating-face appearance.

**H6 wood schedule:** Use 1x6 or 5/4x6 boards and calculate two staggered course sets from actual width and specified overlap. A 6-foot field commonly needs roughly 17-20 boards per bay rather than the 11-14 used by a single-plane horizontal fence. Provide 2x2/2x4 vertical end nailers and a center support on both fastening planes.

**Store request:** `two-plane horizontal 1x6 cedar board-on-board` or `alternating-face horizontal shadowbox`; do not order from the word `horizontal` alone.

### 5.13 Basketweave fence

**Common names:** basketweave, basket weave, woven-board, hurdle-style in some catalogs.

**Exact construction:** Long horizontal boards are flexed alternately in front of and behind one or more vertical **weavers** within a bay. Board ends are captured at posts by paired 2x2 channels or a framed groove. The change in plane at each weaver creates the woven appearance. A top cap restrains and finishes the stack.

**Typical 6-foot x 8-foot bay:**

- 1 allocated heavy 4x4 or 6x6 post;
- 13-14 pieces 1x6x8 horizontal fence board, depending on net framed height;
- paired 2x2 vertical end channels/retainers at the posts;
- 1-3 pieces 2x2x6 vertical weaver/batten, depending on the selected weave count;
- 1 piece 2x4x8 or 2x6x8 top cap/frame;
- optional bottom frame/kickboard.

More weave points require thinner and clearer stock. Knotty, brittle, or thick boards may split when bent. A proprietary basketweave panel or supplier-approved stock is preferable where the bending radius is tight.

**Store request:** `straight-grain 1x6x8 cedar or treated fence boards suitable for basketweave, 2x2 vertical channels and weavers, and a 2x6 top cap.`

**Privacy:** high, with some airflow at the offsets.

### 5.14 Fixed-louver privacy fence

**Common names:** louver, louvre, Venetian, angled-slat privacy.

**Exact construction:** Horizontal or vertical slats are held at a fixed angle in side brackets or notched stiles. Slats overlap the viewing angle to block a direct sightline while leaving airflow passages. A louver is an angled slat; it is not merely a horizontal board with an open gap.

**Common slat stock documented by EZ Louver:**

- 5/4x6 deck boards;
- 1x6 treated or cedar boards;
- 5/8x6 treated or cedar fence pickets.

Their system uses one pair of brackets for every 2 feet of fence height and recommends panel sections no more than 5 feet long unless the system's additional spacers are used.

**Typical 6-foot-high proprietary bay:**

- 1 allocated 4x4/6x6 or compatible metal post;
- 3 pairs of 2-foot louver brackets per panel;
- slat count dictated by the bracket pitch and selected board width;
- matching spacer sets for spans that exceed the base system limit;
- top/bottom frame members if required by the chosen system.

**Store request:** Purchase the louver bracket/stile system first, then buy slats to its approved **actual thickness**, width, and maximum length. Do not estimate slat count from nominal height alone.

**Privacy:** high from the designed view direction, lower from the reverse angle; strong airflow.

### 5.15 Post-and-board / ranch-rail fence

**Common names:** ranch rail, board fence, horse fence, paddock fence, estate rail, 2-board/3-board/4-board.

**Exact construction:** Two, three, or four straight horizontal dimensional-lumber rails are face-fastened to wood posts. Unlike split rail, the rails are sawn rectangular boards and the posts do not need proprietary mortises. Rails are normally stagger-jointed or terminated on post centers.

**R8 schedule:**

- 1 allocated 4x4, 4x6, or 6x6 ground-contact post per added bay;
- 2, 3, or 4 pieces 2x6x8 rail; 2x8 rails are a common heavier option;
- optional welded wire attached on the animal side;
- optional top cap is normally unnecessary because the upper ranch rail is itself exposed.

**Store request:** `ground-contact 4x6 or 6x6 ranch-fence posts at 8 feet O.C. with two/three/four 2x6x8 pressure-treated rails.`

**Visibility:** open. Wire mesh is required when the rail openings are too large for the animal or pet being contained.

**Crossbuck variation:** Add diagonal 2x6 members forming an X between posts, usually with a straight top rail. This is a decorative/equestrian subtype, not a different post system.

### 5.16 Mortised split-rail fence

**Common names:** split rail, zigzag-looking post-and-rail, rustic post-and-rail. Do not confuse it with a freestanding worm/zigzag fence.

**Exact construction:** Proprietary split, hewn, or sawn rails have tapered ends that enter mortises in predrilled posts. Line-post mortises pass through; end-post mortises stop on one face; corner posts have mortises on adjacent faces. Rail geometry and post mortises must come from the same product system.

**SR schedule:**

- 2 or 3 proprietary 8- or 11-foot split rails per bay;
- 1 matching line-post allocation per added bay;
- matching end and corner posts at run conditions;
- optional compatible wire mesh and staples.

Lowe's describes 8- and 11-foot rails, approximately 6-foot posts for two-rail systems, and approximately 7-foot posts for three-rail systems. Home Depot lists a common 3x4x11-foot pressure-treated pine split rail.

**Store request:** `two-rail or three-rail split-rail system with matching line, end, and corner posts`; provide the rail length and species.

**Visibility:** open and rustic.

### 5.17 Round post-and-rail / lodge-pole fence

**Common names:** round rail, lodge-pole, doweled post-and-rail, round post-and-rail.

**Exact construction:** Round rails with machined tenons fit matching holes in round posts. It behaves like split rail but uses turned round components. Half-round rails may instead be face-fastened. Components are system-specific rather than ordinary dimensional lumber.

**Typical schedule:** 2 or 3 round rails per bay, one matching line-post allocation, and purpose-made end/corner posts. Common bay lengths are supplier-specific, often 8-10 feet.

**Store request:** `two-rail/three-rail round post-and-rail system, with tenoned round rails and matching line/end/corner posts.`

### 5.18 Wood-framed welded-wire / hog-wire fence

**Common names:** wood-and-wire, hog-wire, cattle-panel, welded-wire with wood frame, garden fence.

**Exact construction:** Rectangular welded-wire mesh or a rigid livestock panel fills a wood post-and-rail frame. Mesh can be stapled to the inside face, recessed into grooves, or sandwiched between wood cleats. `Hog wire` is informal; specify wire gauge, grid opening, finish, and panel dimensions.

**Typical 4-5-foot x 8-foot bay:**

- 1 allocated 4x4 or 6x6 ground-contact post;
- 1 piece 2x4/2x6 top rail and 1 piece 2x4/2x6 bottom rail;
- optional 2x4 middle rail;
- optional pairs of 1x2 cleats/retainers around the opening;
- optional 2x6 ground-contact kickboard;
- 1 welded-wire panel or cut mesh field sized to the framed opening;
- hot-dip-galvanized or stainless fencing staples/fasteners compatible with the treatment.

**Store request:** `4x4/6x6 ground-contact posts, 2x4 or 2x6 frame rails, 1x2 mesh-retainer cleats, and galvanized welded-wire panel` followed by exact gauge and opening.

**Visibility:** open. This style combines a wood visual frame with pet/garden containment.

### 5.19 Full-lattice / trellis fence

**Common names:** lattice fence, trellis panel, garden screen.

**Exact construction:** A full-height lattice sheet or site-built lattice grid is retained inside a structural perimeter frame between posts. The thin lattice itself is not relied on as the rail or post connection.

**Typical bay:** 4x4 posts, 2x4 top/bottom frame rails, 2x2 or 1x2 lattice retainers, vertical frame stiles as needed, and one exterior-rated lattice field cut to the clear opening.

**Store request:** `exterior wood lattice, square or diagonal opening, with pressure-treated/cedar 2x4 frame and 1x2 or 2x2 lattice retainers.`

**Visibility:** ranges from open decorative to semi-private according to opening size.

### 5.20 Preassembled wood panels

**Common names:** prefab panel, prebuilt panel, factory panel.

**Exact construction:** This is a procurement/installation method, not a visual design. A panel may be solid board, stockade, board-on-board, shadowbox, spaced picket, tongue-and-groove, or lattice-top. The panel already contains rails and infill.

**Per 8-foot panel bay:**

- 1 matching panel;
- 1 allocated post per added bay plus the starting post;
- panel brackets or approved rail-to-post fasteners;
- concrete/gravel or the specified post foundation;
- no separate field rails or pickets unless trimming/repairing the panel.

**Store request:** State height, nominal width, wood species/treatment, panel design, top line, picket profile, and whether the panel is rackable or must be stepped on a slope.

**Modeling rule:** Keep `preassembled` separate from the style ID so the same visual style can be estimated as either component-built or panel-built.

### 5.21 Recognizable regional rail systems

These systems are common enough in particular North American regions to recognize, but they should not be presented as default suburban styles:

- **Slip-board / post-and-slot fence:** Two to four rectangular 2x6 or 2x8 rails drop into routed slots in proprietary wood posts. It resembles ranch rail but uses captured rail ends instead of face-fastened boards. Buy the posts and rails as one compatible system.
- **Worm / zigzag / stack-rail fence:** Long split rails are stacked in interlocking crossed courses at an angle, usually without dug posts. It uses substantially more rail and land width than mortised split rail. Cedar stack rail is also marketed as `battlefield` fence.
- **Buck-and-pole / jackleg fence:** Freestanding crossed timber bucks support horizontal poles or rails. It is used where digging is difficult, including rocky or frozen western sites. Member sizes and bracing are system- and site-specific.

These should have separate planner presets only if acreage, park, agricultural, or historical users are part of the product scope.

## 6. Decorative profiles and modifiers

### Individual picket-top profiles

| Profile | Exact description | Lumber consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Flat / square top** | Straight cut perpendicular to board length. | Buy flat-top boards or crosscut stock. |
| **Dog-ear** | Flat center with both top corners clipped, usually at matching angles. | Buy dog-ear pickets or cut two corners on each board. |
| **Pointed / spear / stockade** | Two diagonal cuts meet at a central point. | Buy pointed pickets; effective wood height includes the point. |
| **Gothic** | Curved shoulders rise to a central point. | Usually a milled fence picket, not a simple straight saw cut. |
| **French Gothic** | More ornate Gothic/fleur-de-lis-like point with concave side cuts. | Buy purpose-milled French Gothic pickets. |

### Overall panel-top lines

| Top line | Exact description | Construction effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Straight / level** | All pickets terminate at the same elevation. | Standard equal-length pickets. |
| **Convex / arched / domed** | Center is higher than both ends. | Varied picket lengths or a template-cut assembled panel. |
| **Concave / scalloped** | Center is lower than both ends. | Varied picket lengths or template cut. |
| **Stepped** | Each bay remains level but adjacent bays change elevation. | Standard rectangular panels work on slopes; posts project differently. |
| **Racked / follows grade** | Rails and/or panel run parallel to the slope. | Component-built vertical pickets remain plumb; not every prefab wood panel can rack. |

### Topper and trim modifiers

- Square, diagonal, or privacy lattice topper.
- Open picket/spindle topper.
- 2x6 or 2x8 cap.
- 1x4/1x6 fascia under the cap.
- Full picture-frame trim.
- Exposed posts, concealed posts, wood-wrapped steel posts, or decorative post caps.
- Alternating board widths or accent strips. These change the coverage formula but not the support system.

## 7. Species and treatment language

Common North American choices include:

- **Western red cedar:** premium, dimensionally stable, naturally decay resistant, widely available in the United States and Canada.
- **Northern white cedar:** common particularly from the Great Lakes through New England and eastern Canada; also used for round post-and-rail.
- **Incense cedar:** common in parts of the western and central United States.
- **Redwood:** regional premium option, especially western United States.
- **Pressure-treated Southern yellow pine:** economical and common from the Midwest/East through the South.
- **Pressure-treated red pine:** regional, especially upper Midwest and parts of Canada.
- **Spruce-pine-fir, spruce, Douglas fir, or hemlock:** regional fence boards/panels; use treatment or a documented exterior system where natural durability is inadequate.

A technically complete lumber line should state:

> quantity + nominal/actual size + length + species/species group + grade if relevant + surfaced/rough texture + moisture condition if relevant + preservative standard/use category + top/profile.

Example:

> 72 pieces, 5/8 in. actual x 5-1/2 in. x 6 ft., Western red cedar fence pickets, No. 2 or better, rough-sawn face, dog-ear top.

Ground-contact example:

> 9 pieces, nominal 4x4x10 ft. pressure-treated posts, treatment end tag marked AWPA UC4A or CSA O80 UC4.1 as applicable.

Do not describe pressure-treated wood only as `green` or `brown`; color is not the use category.

## 8. Exact quantity formulas

Use actual inches in these formulas.

### 8.1 Solid side-by-side boards

`board count = ceiling(covered width / actual board face width)`

For 96 inches and a 5.5-inch board: `ceiling(96 / 5.5) = 18`.

### 8.2 Uniform spaced pickets

For an opening width `B`, actual picket width `W`, and desired gap `G`:

`maximum whole pickets = floor((B + G) / (W + G))`

Recalculate the exact equal gap after selecting the whole-number picket count:

`actual gap = (B - count x W) / (count - 1)`

If equal end margins are required, include two end spaces in the denominator instead of using only the internal gaps.

### 8.3 Board-on-board

Choose required dry-season edge overlap `O`. The largest base-board gap is:

`base gap = cover-board face width - 2 x O`

Then calculate the base course as a spaced-picket course. Cover-board count is normally the number of internal base gaps, adjusted for whether the two bay edges require cover boards.

This is why `19 boards per 8 feet` and `1-inch overlap on both edges` cannot both be assumed without checking the actual layout.

### 8.4 Horizontal courses

For net infill height `H`, actual board width `W`, and gap `G`:

`course count = ceiling((H + G) / (W + G))`

After selecting the count, resolve the top and bottom margins explicitly. Do not silently turn the last gap into a narrow sliver.

### 8.5 Rails

- component-built fence up to 4 feet high: usually 2 rails per bay;
- component-built fence over 4 feet: usually 3 rails per bay;
- specialty framed, louver, basketweave, horizontal, and high-wind systems: use the actual engineered detail rather than this rule of thumb.

### 8.6 Posts

For one straight run divided into `N` bays:

`posts = N + 1`

Then classify rather than double-count:

- first/last posts are end posts;
- direction changes are corner posts;
- gate opening edges are gate posts;
- house/wall terminations may use a terminal post or an approved wall-mounted channel;
- shared corner posts serve both adjoining runs.

### 8.7 Fasteners

For ordinary vertical boards, use two approved fasteners per board at each rail unless the selected board/system manufacturer specifies otherwise:

`board fasteners = board count x rail count x 2`

Thus 18 boards on three rails use 108 board fasteners before waste. Retail guidance lists 2-inch hot-dip-galvanized fencing fasteners for a three-rail, six-fastener-per-picket assembly; other retail guidance allows shorter fasteners when verified against board and rail thickness. The point must not project dangerously through the far rail face.

Rail-to-post connections must be calculated from the chosen joint: face-screwed, toe-screwed, notched, or bracketed. A common face connection uses two 3-inch approved screws at each rail/post bearing, but follow the connector and treatment manufacturers.

### 8.8 Waste and purchasing quantity

After the geometric count, add a visible waste factor rather than hiding it:

- 5% for straight, repetitive runs with good stock;
- 10% for shaped tops, many corners, slopes, sorting knotty boards, or uncertain stock;
- supplier bundle/panel rounding after waste.

The planner should show both `net calculated quantity` and `recommended purchase quantity`.

## 9. Store-ready example order

For 32 linear feet of basic 6-foot solid-board privacy at four 8-foot bays, before local footing engineering and waste:

- 5 pieces 4x4 ground-contact posts, with length selected from required embedment and finished height;
- 12 pieces 2x4x8 exterior fence rails;
- 72 pieces 5/8 x 5-1/2 x 6-foot fence pickets (18 per bay);
- approximately 432 approved 2-inch picket fasteners (six per picket);
- rail-to-post fasteners/connectors for 12 rail pieces and their chosen joints;
- concrete/gravel calculated from actual hole diameter, depth, post displacement, and product yield;
- end-cut preservative compatible with the treated lumber;
- optional 5-10% board and fastener waste.

The post line should not be finalized as `4x4x8` until frost depth, embedment, fence height, top clearance, and local code have been resolved.

### Corrections to the current starter names

- Rename `board_to_board` to `solid_vertical` or display it as **Side-by-side solid-board privacy**. `Board-to-board` is too easily confused with board-on-board.
- The current board-on-board description should not say the boards overlap `on both sides`. Canonical board-on-board uses base and cover courses on the same face of the rails. Alternating opposite faces is shadowbox.
- Keep **good-neighbor** as an alias for shadowbox, but do not assume every locally advertised good-neighbor fence is shadowbox.
- Model **stockade** as solid vertical plus a pointed/dog-ear/Gothic picket profile, while retaining a legacy preset for user recognition.
- Model **cap-and-trim** and **lattice-top** as modifiers that can be applied to multiple privacy patterns.
- Model **preassembled panel** as a procurement method, not a visual construction style.
- A horizontal preset should default to closer post spacing/support than a vertical picket preset and should include a center stiffener/nailer assumption.

## 12. Safety and local-design limitation

This catalog is a material taxonomy and estimating reference, not a stamped structural design. Fence height limits, setbacks, pool-barrier rules, wind exposure, frost depth, utility locating, footing size, post size, gate design, and required permits vary by jurisdiction and site. The planner should expose those assumptions and warn users to confirm them locally rather than presenting one post depth or fastener schedule as universally code-compliant.
