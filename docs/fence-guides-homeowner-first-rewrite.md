# Fence Planner Guides — Homeowner-First Rewrite

This package replaces “how the calculator works” copy with practical help for a person planning a fence for the first time. Keep exact formulas, configuration names, test scenarios, and product-specific notes in an optional **How the estimate works** disclosure or the Methodology page.

## New guide-hub copy

### Title

Fence planning, without the contractor talk

### Introduction

You do not need to know every fence term before you begin. Start with the shape of your yard, the kind of privacy or enclosure you want, and the places people need to pass through. These guides help you make a sensible first plan, understand what the calculator can estimate, and know what still needs a local or product-specific check.

### Reusable page pattern

Every guide should follow this order:

1. **The short answer** — one reassuring sentence in everyday language.
2. **Why it matters** — the cost, fit, or safety mistake this avoids.
3. **Do this first** — three to five simple, physical actions in the yard.
4. **Use Fence Planner for this part** — explain the useful estimate, without claiming the app knows site-specific rules.
5. **Check before buying or digging** — product instructions, property line, permit, utility, or local requirement.
6. **Want the details?** — optional calculation/methodology disclosure.

Use `plain phrase (technical term)` only when the technical term adds value. For example: “the straight section of fence (a run)” or “the middle of one post to the middle of the next (on-center spacing).” Do not put app configuration names, test IDs, or formulas in the first half of a guide.

## Card copy and article rewrites

### 1. How to Measure for a New Fence

**Card description:** Make a simple yard sketch, mark the corners and gate, then measure one straight fence section at a time.

**Short answer:** You do not need a perfect drawing. You need a simple sketch that shows every straight section, every turn, and every place a gate or obstacle changes the fence line.

**Why it matters:** One total distance cannot tell you where corners, posts, gates, or cut panels will land. A few separate measurements can.

**Do this first:**

1. Sketch your house, patio, driveway, and the part of the yard you want to enclose.
2. Put a temporary flag or stake at each start, corner, gate, and end.
3. Pull string between two marks and measure that one straight side. Write its length beside the line on your sketch.
4. Start a new measurement every time the fence turns. Mark the gate width and what needs to fit through it.
5. Note anything that could force a post to move: steps, trees, downspouts, paving, equipment, or a slope.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Draw the same straight sides and gate in the planner. If its shape does not look like your sketch, fix the plan before trusting the material estimate.

**Check before digging:** This is a planning measurement, not a property survey. Confirm the property line, local fence rules, and utilities before any hole is dug.

### 2. How to Calculate Fence Panels and Posts

**Card description:** Turn your yard measurements into a sensible first count of panels and posts—then spot the awkward short section before you shop.

**Short answer:** Count each straight stretch separately. The planner can estimate the panels and posts, but the last section may need a cut panel or a small layout change.

**Why it matters:** An 80-foot fence is rarely just “ten eight-foot panels.” Posts take space, gates split a line in two, and corners are shared by both directions.

**Do this first:**

1. Choose the fence system you are actually considering and find its installation sheet.
2. Add each straight side of your plan separately; place the gate where it will really go.
3. Enter the panel or post-to-post measurement exactly as the product instructions describe it.
4. Look for the final short section. It is normal; it is where a panel may need trimming or the gate position may be worth moving.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** It estimates whole panels to buy and shows where the partial section lands. It is a shopping estimate, not a cut list.

**Check before buying:** Confirm actual panel width, post width, bracket clearance, and cut instructions with the product you choose. Do not cut a panel based only on an estimate.

### 3. Fence Post Spacing Explained

**Card description:** See what “six-foot” and “eight-foot” spacing really mean, and why the number includes the posts at both ends.

**Short answer:** Post spacing is the distance from the middle of one post to the middle of the next. Smaller spacing means more posts, more holes, and usually more support—but follow the chosen fence system’s instructions.

**Why it matters:** On a simple 96-foot straight fence, eight-foot spacing uses 13 posts; six-foot spacing uses 17. That is four extra posts and four extra holes before gates or corners are added.

**Do this first:**

1. Find the spacing required by the panel, picket, or chain-link system you want.
2. Mark only the first and last post position on the ground.
3. Use the planner to compare spacing choices on the same line.
4. Treat gates, corners, and ends as special locations, not ordinary middle posts.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Compare a few plausible layouts before buying materials. The planner can show the difference in post count and a likely short final section.

**Check before buying:** Fence height, wind exposure, soil, and the specific product can change what is suitable. A common spacing is not a structural rule.

### 4. How Much Concrete Does Each Fence Post Need?

**Card description:** Estimate concrete from the hole size, the actual post size, and the bag yield printed on the product—not a generic “bags per post” rule.

**Short answer:** Start with the hole size your local requirements and fence system call for, then let the planner estimate the concrete for all matching posts together.

**Why it matters:** A deeper or wider hole uses much more concrete. Gate and corner posts may need a different detail from ordinary line posts, and bag yields vary by product.

**Do this first:**

1. Separate the posts that will use different hole sizes or footing details.
2. Measure the actual post cross-section or use the manufacturer’s listed size.
3. Enter the chosen hole diameter, depth, and the concrete yield printed on the bag.
4. Add a modest contingency if your holes are likely to vary, then round the project total up to whole bags.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** It gives a transparent starting total for a cylindrical hole. It rounds the entire group rather than needlessly rounding every post into extra bags.

**Check before digging:** The app does not know your frost depth, soil, wind exposure, drainage, gate loads, or local code. Verify those details first; do not use a shallow hole just because rock is encountered.

### 5. How to Plan Fence Corners, Ends, and Terminal Posts

**Card description:** Mark each turn, end, and gate before counting posts, so one shared corner does not become two accidental posts.

**Short answer:** When the fence turns a corner, both directions usually share one post at that turn. Start by marking the unique places where the fence begins, ends, turns, or opens for a gate.

**Why it matters:** Counting every line separately can make you buy and dig for duplicate posts. It also hides the places that may need a stronger product-specific assembly.

**Do this first:**

1. Draw every fence line until it touches the next one.
2. Circle each open end, corner, and side of a gate once.
3. Check whether the fence ends near a house, shed, driveway, or another obstacle.
4. Choose the fence system before deciding what hardware or bracing a corner needs.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** The planner uses the connected layout to avoid counting one corner twice. Review the marked points before treating the material list as final.

**Check before building:** Wood, vinyl, and chain-link corners are built differently. A wall connection is not automatically a safe substitute for a freestanding end post; use a verified detail for the product and structure.

### 6. How to Measure and Plan a Fence Gate

**Card description:** Plan the clear passage first, then choose a gate product, its post opening, and a swing path that does not hit the yard.

**Short answer:** Start with what must pass through the gate. Then choose a gate system and set the posts using that product’s required opening—not simply the width you hope the gate leaf will be.

**Why it matters:** A gate that clears a person may not clear bins, a mower, or a vehicle. A gate that fits on paper may still swing into steps, a slope, or landscaping.

**Do this first:**

1. Measure the widest thing that needs to pass through the opening.
2. Choose the gate product before setting posts; its instructions define the post opening and hardware clearance.
3. Mark both posts and walk the swing path with string or a board.
4. Check the remaining fence sections on both sides of the gate.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Place the gate on its real fence line and compare a few positions. Moving it can make the remaining sections more balanced, even when the total panel count stays the same.

**Check before building:** Follow the gate manufacturer’s instructions for post, hinge, latch, clearance, and support requirements. Make sure the swing does not block a path, stairs, vehicle, or required access.

### 7. Wood Panels vs. Individual Pickets

**Card description:** Choose prebuilt panels for speed or individual pickets for flexibility—then plan materials around the system you will actually install.

**Short answer:** Panels are faster to install on a simple, level run. Individual pickets give you more control around slopes, unusual lengths, and custom designs.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Create the layout first. Then use the panel option for a prefabricated system or a picket-based estimate for a site-built fence. Keep the two shopping lists separate.

**Check before buying:** Confirm rails, brackets, picket gaps, fasteners, and slope limits with the system you choose.

### 8. How to Plan a Fence on Sloped Ground

**Card description:** Decide whether your fence will step down the hill or follow the slope before buying panels or placing posts.

**Short answer:** A stepped fence uses level sections with drops between them. A racked fence follows the grade. They look and measure differently, so choose the style before finalizing materials.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Use the plan to map the horizontal route and mark the changes in grade. Keep a separate note of the rise and the fence product’s slope limit.

**Check before buying:** Not every panel can rack, and a steep yard may need short sections, custom work, or an approved alternative. Follow product instructions.

### 9. Privacy Fence Materials Checklist

**Card description:** Build a complete privacy-fence shopping list in the order the work happens: posts, concrete, frame, boards, gate, and finishing items.

**Short answer:** A privacy fence needs more than panels and posts. Build your list by system, then check it against your final layout before going to the store.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Export or copy the estimate, then add product-specific rails, brackets, fasteners, caps, gates, and finishing supplies from the installation instructions.

### 10. Chain-Link Fence Materials Checklist

**Card description:** Turn a simple chain-link layout into a list of fabric, line posts, terminals, rail, fittings, gates, and tension parts.

**Short answer:** Chain-link uses a different set of parts from a wood privacy fence. Begin with a specific system; terminal posts and fittings matter as much as the fabric.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Use it for the route, length, gates, and first pass at post locations. Build the final takeoff from the selected manufacturer’s component list.

### 11. Fence Installation Order

**Card description:** Use a safe planning sequence before you start digging, but follow the installation order supplied for your specific fence system.

**Short answer:** First confirm the line and utilities. Then set out the posts, install the system as its manufacturer directs, and leave finishing details until the structure is stable.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Treat the final layout and shopping list as a pre-build check, not as installation instructions.

### 12. Common Fence-Planning Mistakes

**Card description:** Catch the expensive planning mistakes before buying: one total measurement, forgotten gates, duplicate corner posts, and unverified local rules.

**Short answer:** Most avoidable fence mistakes happen before the first hole. Walk through this short check before you order materials.

**Checklist:** Confirm the line, locate utilities, sketch each straight section, choose the gate product, check the last short section, and compare the plan with your yard one more time.

### 13. Fence Permit and Property-Line Checklist

**Card description:** Check the boundary, height rules, setbacks, permits, HOA rules, and easements before your layout becomes a purchase.

**Short answer:** Do this before you shop. A well-calculated fence can still be wrong if it is in the wrong place or the wrong height.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Use it after you know the limits of the buildable area. The planner estimates a layout; it cannot approve a property line or permit.

### 14. How to Mark Underground Utilities Before Digging

**Card description:** Request utility locates before every post hole, read the paperwork, and treat every mark as a safety boundary—not a hint.

**Short answer:** Do not dig from a planner diagram alone. Request the required utility locate, wait for every response, and follow the locating authority’s instructions.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Adjust the proposed layout when a confirmed utility or required clearance affects a post location. Never assume a small shift makes digging safe.

### 15. Fence Project Shopping List

**Card description:** Turn a material estimate into a store-ready list, grouped by the actual fence system and with room to confirm product dimensions and prices.

**Short answer:** Bring a list that separates posts, fill, concrete, gates, hardware, tools, and finishing items. Do not substitute a similarly named product without checking its installation dimensions.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Start with the estimate, then add the manufacturer-specific components and a small, labelled allowance for normal cuts or variation.

### 16. How to Estimate Fence Waste

**Card description:** Add a deliberate, visible allowance for cuts and breakage instead of guessing—or hiding extra material in every line item.

**Short answer:** Waste is a planning allowance, not a rule. A square, simple fence may need little extra; a fence with many cuts, slopes, or custom details may need more.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Keep the base estimate visible, then show the allowance separately so you can see what you are choosing to buy extra.

### 17. How to Plan a Fence Around a House or Existing Structure

**Card description:** Leave room for maintenance, drainage, doors, downspouts, and access before ending a fence near a house, shed, deck, or other structure.

**Short answer:** Do not force a fence line into a structure because it looks tidy on the plan. End it where there is enough room to build, use, and maintain both things safely.

**Use Fence Planner for this part:** Sketch the structure as an obstacle, then try a freestanding end post and a clear gap first. Treat a structural connection as an exception that needs a verified detail.

## Diagram policy

Use one instructional diagram only where it answers a real beginner question. Every diagram should have:

- one physical scene or simple plan view;
- no more than three teaching points at once;
- plain-English labels first, optional technical term in parentheses;
- a caption that states the action to take;
- no formulas, IDs, or app settings inside the image.

The six accompanying SVGs replace the existing taxonomy-heavy diagrams. They are under `outputs/guides/diagrams/` and retain the destination filenames used by the current guide model.
